Introduction

The NYC311 initiative was launched in 2003 by then-Mayor Michael Bloomberg as a clearinghouse for all government-related enquiries. Known as ‘the agency that never sleeps’, the NYC311 Call Center not only operates 24/7, but maintains a good service standard by treating all calls equally regardless of the time it is made. In 2016 alone, NYC311 has received 36 million customer contacts; of which about 2.4 million was reported as service requests. A quick count revealed that this number has been increasing throughout the years. Service requests could be seen as an expression of dissatisfaction, or otherwise known as complaint, as it means that New Yorkers have to take the trouble to alert the government to solve issues.



Just who are these people making the complaints? Where are they located at? When are people complaining? Various websites have attempted to provide visualisation using NYC311 open data:

  • Mapping NYC311 Noise Complaints - this site allows user to interactively explore various types of noise complaints on a map.
  • NYC311 Data Exploration - this site by NYC311 allows user to spatially view the total number of requests in each community district on a map.
  • Summer of 311 - this static map illustrates the most common complaint by neighbourhood.

However, these sites do not allow users to interactively explore the NYC311 data according to its spatial and temporal distribution and at the neighbourhood level and do not display additional information on the neighbourhood. Dissatisfaction is an antecedent of complaining but it along may not always drive a person to log a complaint (Landon, 1980). Income was found to be positively correlated to complaining behaviour (Warland, Hermann and Willitis, 1975). This website seeks to explore whether this finding holds for NYC by combining various data sources . Using the 'Map' section, you can interactively explore the complaints data and the affluence of the neighbourhoods using maps and charts. In the 'Analysis' section, you can interactively explore the relationships between concentration of complaints made and affluence at the neighbourhood level, and the various clusters of complaints in NYC as a whole.

Due to the sheer number and variety of complaints, this website will only explore the top 10 complaint types.

References
